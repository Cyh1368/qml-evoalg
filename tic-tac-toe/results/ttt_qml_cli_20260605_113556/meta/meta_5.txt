# GLOBAL INSIGHTS SCRATCHPAD

The following are global insights about optimization approaches and their effectiveness:

## Successful Algorithmic Patterns  
- Compact, repeated single-qubit rotations with minimal distinct parameters  
  • Generation 0’s ANSATZ_SPEC uses only three tied RY parameters (ry_0 on even perimeter wires, ry_1 on odd perimeter wires, and ry_8 at the center) per block, yielding 18 total trainable parameters and the best combined score 0.50 (vs 0.43–0.40 in later gens).  
- Shallow, uniform entanglement on the tic-tac-toe grid  
  • A single CZ layer on all adjacent grid edges per block (circuit depth 58, 153 gates) produced higher accuracy (~0.49 validation) than deeper or denser entanglers in other variants.  
- Re-uploading feature map between ansatz blocks  
  • All top performers re-encoded the RX feature map three times, but Gen 0’s combination of 3 uploads × 2 repeats balanced expressivity and trainability most effectively.  

## Ineffective Approaches  
- Adding parameterized two-qubit gates (CRX/CRZ) on grid edges  
  • Gen 1 (42 params, depth 72) dropped to combined 0.43; Gen 2 (42 params, depth 78) fell to 0.40; Gen 3 (24 params, depth 72) only 0.44; Gen 4 (30 params, depth 118) also 0.44. None outperformed the simple CZ-only entanglement of Gen 0.  
- Increasing circuit depth and gate count without structural insight  
  • Raising depth from 58 to 72–118 and gates from 153 to 177–231 did not improve—and often hurt—validation accuracy (down to 31–38%), indicating diminishing returns from deeper, denser layouts.  
- Full per-wire parameter diversity in single-qubit layers  
  • Gen 2’s unique RY/RZ per wire led to underfitting (validation 0.31) despite 231 gates; over-parameterization without guided tying degraded performance.  

## Implementation Insights  
- Parameter-tying topology matters more than sheer parameter count  
  • Alternating ry_0/ry_1 on perimeter wires plus ry_8 at center (Gen 0) achieved better generalization (gap 0.02) than fully unique or fully shared schemes in later gens.  
- CZ gate uniformity maintains symmetry without blow-up in depth  
  • A single CZ pass on all grid edges per block preserves the problem’s graph structure and avoids the noise/overhead of extra CRX/CRZ layers.  
- Balanced sampling with replacement keeps dataset size while controlling class imbalance  
  • Gen 0’s sampling (450 train, 600 test) with replacement maintained class balance and ensured no train–test leakage beyond 2% overlap, supporting a low generalization gap.  

## Performance Analysis  
- Best vs. next best combined score  
  • Gen 0: combined 0.50 at depth 58, 18 params, 153 gates.  
  • Gen 3: combined 0.44 at depth 72, 24 params, 177 gates.  
- Depth/performance trade-off  
  • Every increase in depth beyond 58 (to 72, 78, 118) coincided with a 0.06–0.10 drop in combined score, despite added parametric expressivity.  
- Parameter efficiency correlates with structured tying  
  • Although Gen 3 matches Gen 0’s parameter efficiency (0.02), its lower overall accuracy (≈0.38 vs. Gen 0’s 0.43 test) underscores that efficiency alone isn’t sufficient—parameter layout and entangling pattern are critical.

# META RECOMMENDATIONS

The following are actionable recommendations for the next program generations:

1. Introduce a corner–edge–center tying topology for your RY layer: replace the alternating ry_0/ry_1 perimeter scheme with three tied parameters—“ry_corner” on wires {0,2,4,6}, “ry_edge” on {1,3,5,7}, and “ry_center” on wire 8. This still yields 3 parameters per block (18 total) but aligns more directly with the grid’s geometric symmetries, potentially improving generalization.  

2. Augment each ansatz block with a single global RZ rotation after the CZ layer: add qml.RZ(param="rz", wires=wire) on every qubit, where “rz” is a new per-block scalar parameter. This injects extra phase flexibility without blowing up parameter count (now 4 per block, 24 total) and preserves the shallow, uniform structure that drove Gen 0’s success.  

3. Between the two repeats in each upload, re-encode the feature map: within the upload loop do feature_map → block → feature_map → block instead of only at the upload boundaries. This mid-block re-upload reinforces input information and may boost expressivity while keeping circuit depth close to 58 per upload cycle.  

4. Vary the CZ entanglement pattern across blocks by splitting GRID_EDGES into two disjoint matchings (e.g. alternating “horizontal + vertical” vs “star” around the center) and apply only one matching per block in alternation. You’ll maintain a single CZ layer per block (153 gates overall) but introduce richer correlations over successive layers.  

5. Swap the upload/repeat schedule to 4 uploads × 1 repeat (still 4 blocks) or 2 uploads × 3 repeats (6 blocks), keeping total blocks constant at 6. This tests whether more frequent re-uploads with fewer repeats (or vice versa) better balances feature encoding and variational depth, building directly on the 3×2 split that proved optimal in Gen 0.
