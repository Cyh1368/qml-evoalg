# GLOBAL INSIGHTS SCRATCHPAD

The following are global insights about optimization approaches and their effectiveness:

## Successful Algorithmic Patterns  
- Parameterized CRX perimeter ring + shared-parameter CRY hub entanglement  
  • Generation 10 (Combined 0.60, train 0.57 → val 0.65 → test 0.50) outperformed both the static-CZ ring/CRZ-hub pattern of the prior best (Combined 0.57) and the mixed two-qubit patterns in Gen 12 (0.55) and Gen 15 (0.55).  
- Five-layer hierarchical structure (pre-entangle RZ → RY → entangle → post-entangle RZ)  
  • All top scorers—including Gen 10 (0.60), Gen 12 (0.55), and Gen 13 (0.56)—use this rotation–entanglement–rotation block, confirming its role in balancing expressivity and trainability.  
- Fixed 3 uploads × 2 repeats backbone (depth 70, gate 261)  
  • Retaining N_UPLOADS=3 and N_REPEATS=2 yielded consistent depth/gate counts across the best programs and correlated with Combined scores ≥ 0.55.  
- Symmetry-driven parameter sharing  
  • Grouping corners/edges/center for pre-RZ, RY, and post-RZ layers plus single shared CRX-ring and CRY-hub angles kept N_PARAMS per block at 11 (total 66) and hit a performance sweet spot.  

## Ineffective Approaches  
- Mixing multiple two-qubit parameter types on ring or hub  
  • Gen 12’s ring-CRX + hub-CRX/CRY (72 params) and Gen 15’s perimeter-CZ + hub-CRZ/CRX (66 params) both fell to Combined 0.55, indicating that extra gate varieties dilute useful expressivity.  
- Excessive parameter count and depth increases  
  • Gen 13 doubled N_PARAMS to 84 and depth to 132 but only reached Combined 0.56, showing diminishing returns from added complexity.  
- Over-consolidated single-qubit sharing  
  • Gen 14 cut N_PARAMS to 48 by merging too many RZ layers and dropped to Combined 0.47 with a 0.17 generalization gap, evidence of underfitting from overly aggressive sharing.  
- Parameterized perimeter CRZ (Gen 6)  
  • As previously observed, adding perimetric CRZ parameters (66 params) yielded only Combined 0.53 versus 0.57 with static CZ, reinforcing that perimeter parameters must be judicious.  

## Implementation Insights  
- Centralized ANSATZ_SPEC builder enables fast gate-type swaps  
  • In Gen 10, changing perimeter CRZ→CRX and hub CRZ→CRY was a two-line edit in `_build_hierarchical_ansatz_spec()`, directly driving the +0.03 Combined improvement over prior best.  
- Ordered parameter-key mapping simplifies multi-upload indexing  
  • Building `PARAMETER_KEYS_PER_BLOCK` via `dict.fromkeys` ensured each of the 11 shared params per block was consistently placed, yielding N_PARAMS_PER_BLOCK=11 and total N_PARAMS=66.  
- Early spec validation prevents wasted runs  
  • `validate_ansatz_spec` caught no GRID_EDGE_SET violations in Gen 10, in contrast to past diagonal entanglement errors (Gen 5).  
- AnnotatedQueue depth/gate measurement ties design to metrics  
  • Using `qml.queuing.AnnotatedQueue` confirmed Gen 10’s depth 70/gate 261, matching the performance sweet spot identified across generations.  

## Performance Analysis  
- Combined score peak at 66 parameters  
  • Gen 10 (66 params) achieved Combined 0.60, surpassing Gen 1’s 60 params/0.57 and Gen 12/15’s 66–72 params/0.55. Going above 66 (Gen 13) or below 66 (Gen 14) degrades performance.  
- Depth stagnation beyond 70 gates is wasteful  
  • Deeper circuits (Gen 13 depth 132) did not raise Combined above 0.56; depth 70 remains optimal.  
- Highest validation accuracy aligns with expressive entanglement  
  • Gen 10’s val 0.65 is the best seen, trading a slightly larger generalization gap (0.07) against underfitting variants (gaps ≤ 0.03 but lower val scores).  
- Parameter efficiency sweet spot ~0.009  
  • Gen 10 test 0.50/66 params ≈ 0.0076, topping Gen 12 (0.45/72 ≈ 0.0063) and Gen 1 (0.48/60 ≈ 0.0080), confirming that 66 well-placed parameters yield the strongest overall balance.

# META RECOMMENDATIONS

The following are actionable recommendations for the next program generations:

1. Insert a mid‐block phase‐alignment rotation using existing RZ keys. In `_build_hierarchical_ansatz_spec`, immediately after the eight perimeter CRX gates (Layer 3), append RZ rotations on corners, edges, and center—but reuse the existing `rz_pre_corner`, `rz_pre_edge`, and `rz_pre_center` parameters rather than introducing new ones. This leverages proven symmetry‐driven sharing to condition the ring outputs before the hub entanglement, preserves N_PARAMS=11/block, and tests whether an extra rotation before the CRY layer boosts validation accuracy.  

2. Swap the two‐qubit rotation axes between the ring and hub. In the ANSATZ_SPEC builder, change the perimeter entanglement gates from CRX→CRY and the hub gates from CRY→CRX, keeping the same parameter keys (`crx_ring`→`cry_ring`, `cry_hub_edge`→`crx_hub_edge`). This two‐line edit maintains depth 70 and 11 params per block while exploring whether reversing axis assignments improves expressivity without over‐parameterizing.  

3. Enrich non-local coupling with diagonal entanglement under the same budget. After the hub CRY layer in each block, append two additional CRY gates on diagonals (wires [0,4] and [2,6]) using the existing `cry_hub_edge` key. This symmetry-preserving tweak adds cross-board correlations—confirmed important for high validation accuracy—without increasing the total parameter count.  

4. Reorder entanglement layers to test early global coupling. Modify `_build_hierarchical_ansatz_spec` so that the four hub CRY gates (Layer 4) occur immediately after the RY rotations (Layer 2) and before the CRX ring (Layer 3). Because depth, gate count, and parameters remain unchanged, this block‐level reordering can reveal whether introducing central entanglement before perimeter coupling accelerates convergence or raises validation performance.  

5. Lightly tighten parameter sharing on the center qubit. Replace both `rz_pre_center` and `rz_post_center` with a single shared key `rz_center` in the spec, applying it to the center wire in both pre- and post-entanglement RZ layers. This reduces N_PARAMS per block from 11→10 (total 60→66→…→60), testing whether focused center‐axis efficiency preserves a high combined score while improving parameter efficiency.
